New York · Mental Hygiene Law Article 81 · general overview

Understanding Article 81 guardianship

A plain-language overview of what Article 81 is, how the process generally works, the alternatives that may accomplish the same goal, and everyone's rights in it. The same information is shown to every reader — nothing here is based on your own facts.

What Article 81 is

Article 81 of New York's Mental Hygiene Law lets a court appoint a guardian to make decisions about the personal needs and/or property and finances of an adult the court finds cannot manage those things for themselves.§81.01 [verify] The person the case is about is called the alleged incapacitated person, or "AIP."

New York law favors the least-restrictive approach — a guardianship is meant to be tailored only to the specific areas where help is genuinely needed, and only when a lesser alternative won't do.§81.02 [verify] Article 81 is a court proceeding — not a do-it-yourself matter; the sections below describe how it generally works so you can have an informed conversation with an attorney.

How the process generally works

A general picture of the shape of an Article 81 case. This is not a checklist of steps to take, and it deliberately omits court, form, fee, and filing specifics — those depend on the situation and belong with an attorney.

GENERALLY
A case is started
Someone asks a court to consider whether a guardian is needed for the AIP.
GENERALLY
A court evaluator
The court typically appoints a neutral evaluator to look into the situation and report back.§81.09 [verify]
GENERALLY
A hearing
The court holds a hearing where the AIP has rights and can take part.§81.11 [verify]
GENERALLY
A decision
The court decides whether a guardian is needed and, if so, exactly which powers are granted.§81.15 [verify]
What a case involves — according to the New York courts

The New York State Unified Court System sets out how a case starts. A petitioner generally files three court forms, then serves them:NY Courts · How to Start an Article 81 Case [verify]

NY COURTS
Petition
A written request explaining why a guardian is believed necessary, who should serve, and for what.
NY COURTS
Order to show cause
Notifies the AIP, states their legal rights, and sets the hearing date.
NY COURTS
Request for judicial intervention
Asks that a judge be assigned to the case.

By statute, the petition must contain specific information — New York Mental Hygiene Law §81.08 lists what it must include, sworn under oath:MHL §81.08 [verify]

  • the AIP’s functional level — ability to manage daily living, and understanding of the consequences
  • specific facts said to show the person is likely to suffer harm
  • the particular powers sought and how they relate to the person’s needs
  • the person’s approximate financial resources and any benefits
  • the people to be notified (family / interested persons) and the proposed guardian
  • the less-restrictive alternatives already considered, and whether they are sufficient

You are not legally required to have a lawyer to file — the New York courts publish self-help materials and DIY resources — but the courts call it a serious matter and offer ways to find counsel. Alternatives that may accomplish the same goal

Because guardianship is meant to be a last resort, it helps to know the common less-restrictive alternatives. Which (if any) fits a given person is a decision for that person, their family, and an attorney — not something this page decides.

Supported decision-making
The person keeps legal authority and chooses trusted supporters to help them understand choices and communicate decisions.
Power of attorney
A person who still has capacity authorizes someone to handle financial matters for them.
Health-care proxy
Names someone to make health-care decisions if the person can't make them for themselves.
Trusts & representative payee
Arrangements that let someone manage specific assets or benefit income without a full guardianship.
Everyone's rights in the process

Article 81 is about protecting a person — so the AIP has strong rights. Whether you are considering a petition or you are the person a petition concerns (or their family), these apply:

  • The AIP has the right to their own attorney, and the court can appoint one (counsel or a court evaluator) to represent their interests.§81.10 [verify]
  • The AIP has the right to be present and to participate in the hearing.§81.11 [verify]
  • The AIP has the right to object and to contest whether a guardian is needed or which powers are granted.
  • Any guardianship the court grants is meant to be limited to what's actually needed, preserving the person's independence everywhere else.§81.02 [verify]
